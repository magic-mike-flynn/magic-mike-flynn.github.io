Every GEO audit I run starts the same way. I pull up the site's structured data, and I find the same three types: Organization, WebSite, BreadcrumbList. Maybe a FAQPage if someone's SEO team was ambitious. That's it.

Then I open Schema.org's full type hierarchy, and it's like discovering your toolbox has 800 tools but you've only ever used the hammer, the screwdriver, and the tape measure.

Schema.org defines types for financial products, medical organizations, legal services, software applications, educational courses, recipes, events, vehicles, job postings, and dozens of other specific categories. These aren't obscure experimental types. They're well-documented, supported by Google's structured data guidelines, and — critically — they're what AI models look for when they need to form confident recommendations.

Most companies don't know they exist.

## The Default Three

Here's what a typical well-maintained site has:

```json
{ "@type": "Organization", "name": "Acme Corp", "url": "..." }
{ "@type": "WebSite", "url": "...", "potentialAction": { "@type": "SearchAction" } }
{ "@type": "BreadcrumbList", "itemListElement": [...] }
```

These are fine. They're table stakes. They tell AI models "this is a company with a website that has pages." Which is roughly as useful as telling a hiring manager "I am a person who has a resume."

The Organization type says you exist. It doesn't say what you do. It doesn't describe your products in a way that machines can compare against competitors. It doesn't give an AI model the structured properties it needs to answer "what's the best X for Y?"

And that's where the 797 other types come in.

## What Your Industry Has That You're Not Using

### Finance: FinancialProduct, FinancialService, BankAccount, InvestmentFund

When someone asks an AI "what's the best savings account for high-interest rates?" the model is looking for structured data it can parse into a comparison. Not marketing copy about "competitive rates" — actual machine-readable properties.

Schema.org's `FinancialProduct` type has properties for `annualPercentageRate`, `feesAndCommissionsSpecification`, `interestRate`, and `currency`. `BankAccount` adds `bankAccountType` and `accountMinimumInflow`. `InvestmentFund` includes `investmentType` and `fundMinimum`.

In our audit of Wealthsimple (GEO score: 75/100), they had solid Organization and FinancialService schema on their homepage. But their individual investment product pages — the ones with fee schedules, account types, and eligibility requirements that AI models love — had minimal structured data. The content existed. The properties were right there on the page. They just weren't wrapped in the schema types that make them machine-extractable.

The gap between having fee information in an HTML table and having it in a `FinancialProduct` schema with explicit `feesAndCommissionsSpecification` is the gap between an AI model guessing at your fees and citing them confidently.

### Healthcare: MedicalOrganization, MedicalClinic, Physician, Drug, MedicalCondition

Healthcare is maybe the most under-schemaed industry relative to what's available. Schema.org has `MedicalOrganization` with properties like `medicalSpecialty` and `availableService`. `Physician` includes `medicalSpecialty`, `hospitalAffiliation`, and `availableService`. `Drug` has `activeIngredient`, `dosageForm`, `drugClass`, and `prescriptionStatus`.

When someone asks an AI "what dermatologists near me accept Blue Cross?" the model needs structured data about medical specialties, insurance acceptance, and location. A healthcare provider with only Organization schema is invisible to this query. One with `MedicalClinic` schema including `medicalSpecialty: "Dermatology"` and `paymentAccepted: "Blue Cross"` is directly answerable.

Most healthcare providers' structured data looks like it was generated by a WordPress plugin that knows nothing about healthcare. Generic Organization schema with a name, address, and phone number. The same markup you'd use for a dry cleaner.

### Legal: LegalService, Attorney

`LegalService` lets you specify `areaServed`, the types of legal matters handled, and connect to `Attorney` types with `practiceArea` properties. When someone asks an AI "what are the best employment lawyers in Toronto?" the model can directly match queries to structured practice areas and jurisdictions.

Our Clio audit (legal practice management, GEO score: 70/100) showed this pattern clearly. Strong content library, solid authority in legal tech — but WordPress-generated schema doing the minimum. Nothing that told AI models "this is a legal technology platform serving lawyers who practice in these areas."

### SaaS: SoftwareApplication

This is the big one, and the most commonly missed. `SoftwareApplication` has properties for `applicationCategory`, `applicationSubCategory`, `operatingSystem`, `softwareRequirements`, `downloadUrl`, `featureList`, `screenshot`, and `offers` with full pricing structure.

Deel, our highest-scoring audit (82/100), had Organization and WebSite schema on their homepage and Product schema on their pricing page. Zero `SoftwareApplication` instances. They're a SaaS platform serving 40,000+ customers across 150+ countries, and nothing in their structured data says "this is software."

When an AI answers "best global payroll software," it can build confident responses from sites that have `SoftwareApplication` schema with `applicationCategory: "BusinessApplication"` and `applicationSubCategory: "Human Resources Software"`. Sites without it are relying on the AI to infer the category from unstructured content — which it can do, but with less confidence, and with a higher chance of misclassification.

## The Confidence Gap

Here's why this matters beyond just "more schema is better."

AI models have an internal confidence level about every claim they make. When a model says "Deel is a global payroll platform," it's drawing on thousands of training data points and whatever it can retrieve from the web. The confidence is high because there's lots of consistent information.

When a model says "Deel's pricing starts at $49/month for contractors," it's less confident — unless it can find that specific data point in structured, machine-readable format. And if it's not confident, one of two things happens: it hedges ("pricing varies, check the website") or it confabulates (states a number that might be outdated or wrong).

Structured data with specific types and properties directly feeds the confidence mechanism. `SoftwareApplication` with `offers: { price: "49", priceCurrency: "USD" }` is an unambiguous signal. A pricing page with "$49/mo" in an H3 tag surrounded by marketing copy is an inference.

The difference between unambiguous signals and inferences is the difference between getting recommended and getting mentioned-with-caveats.

## Why Everyone Defaults to the Same Three

There are practical reasons the structured data landscape looks so uniform:

**CMS plugins generate generic schema.** Yoast, RankMath, and similar SEO plugins add Organization, WebSite, and BreadcrumbList automatically. They don't know you're a fintech company that should have FinancialProduct schema or a healthcare provider that needs MedicalOrganization. They give everyone the same baseline and call it done.

**Schema.org's documentation is overwhelming.** The full hierarchy has 800+ types. Finding the right one for your industry requires navigating a tree that starts at "Thing" and branches into increasingly specific subcategories. Most developers look at the hierarchy, find Organization, and stop looking.

**Nobody's measuring schema specificity.** Traditional SEO tools check whether you have structured data and whether it's valid. They don't check whether you're using the most specific type available. You can pass every structured data validator with just Organization and WebSite — because those are valid. They're just not useful for AI recommendation.

**The ROI wasn't clear until AI search.** Before AI models started using structured data for recommendations, the main benefit of rich schema was Google Rich Results — knowledge panels, review stars, FAQ dropdowns. Those features work fine with generic types. The AI recommendation use case is the first where type specificity directly impacts visibility.

## The Implementation Is Easier Than You Think

Here's the part that frustrates me about this gap: implementing industry-specific schema isn't hard. It's the same JSON-LD injection pattern as Organization schema. You're just using different types and properties.

Adding `SoftwareApplication` schema to a SaaS product page:

```json
{
  "@context": "https://schema.org",
  "@type": "SoftwareApplication",
  "name": "Your Product",
  "applicationCategory": "BusinessApplication",
  "applicationSubCategory": "Your Specific Category",
  "operatingSystem": "Web-based",
  "offers": {
    "@type": "Offer",
    "price": "99",
    "priceCurrency": "USD",
    "billingIncrement": "Monthly"
  },
  "featureList": ["Feature 1", "Feature 2", "Feature 3"],
  "aggregateRating": {
    "@type": "AggregateRating",
    "ratingValue": "4.8",
    "reviewCount": "500"
  }
}
```

Adding `FinancialProduct` to a banking product page:

```json
{
  "@context": "https://schema.org",
  "@type": "FinancialProduct",
  "name": "High-Interest Savings Account",
  "annualPercentageRate": "4.5",
  "feesAndCommissionsSpecification": "No monthly fees, no minimum balance",
  "interestRate": {
    "@type": "QuantitativeValue",
    "value": "4.5",
    "unitCode": "P1"
  },
  "provider": {
    "@type": "FinancialService",
    "name": "Your Bank"
  }
}
```

Adding `MedicalClinic` to a healthcare provider page:

```json
{
  "@context": "https://schema.org",
  "@type": "MedicalClinic",
  "name": "Your Clinic",
  "medicalSpecialty": "Dermatology",
  "availableService": {
    "@type": "MedicalProcedure",
    "name": "Skin Cancer Screening"
  },
  "paymentAccepted": "Blue Cross, Aetna, United Healthcare",
  "address": { ... }
}
```

Each of these is 15 minutes of work for a developer who knows JSON-LD. Even if you need to template it across hundreds of pages, it's a one-time pattern that your CMS can apply to every page in a category.

The effort is measured in hours. The impact is measured in whether AI models can confidently recommend your specific products, services, and features — or whether they're guessing from unstructured text.

## How to Find Your Missing Types

**Step 1:** Go to [schema.org/docs/full.html](https://schema.org/docs/full.html) and search for your industry keyword. Type "financial," "medical," "legal," "software," "education," or whatever describes your domain.

**Step 2:** Find the most specific type that matches your business. Don't use Organization if MedicalOrganization exists. Don't use Product if SoftwareApplication fits. Don't use Service if LegalService is accurate. Schema.org rewards specificity — the more specific the type, the more specific the properties available, and the more structured data you can provide.

**Step 3:** Check the properties available on that type. This is where the gold is. Each specific type has properties that generic types don't. `FinancialProduct` has `annualPercentageRate`. `SoftwareApplication` has `featureList`. `MedicalClinic` has `medicalSpecialty`. These properties are what AI models extract for comparisons.

**Step 4:** Validate with Google's Rich Results Test. Not all Schema.org types generate Rich Results, but the validation confirms your JSON-LD is well-formed. Then test with AI models directly — ask ChatGPT or Perplexity a question your schema should help answer, and see if the response improves.

## The Competitive Window

Right now, in April 2026, almost nobody in most industries is using vertical-specific schema types for AI visibility purposes. They're using generic types because that's what their CMS generates and what their SEO tool validates.

This is a window. The companies that implement industry-specific schema now get a structural advantage in AI recommendations before their competitors realize the gap exists. The first fintech company in a category to have `FinancialProduct` schema with complete properties gets recommended with higher confidence. The first healthcare provider in a market with `MedicalClinic` schema including specialties and insurance gets matched to queries their competitors can't.

And unlike content creation — which takes months — schema implementation takes days. It's the fastest path to improved AI visibility for companies that already have the content but aren't wrapping it in the right machine-readable types.

Eight hundred types. You're using three. The other 797 aren't all relevant to you — but somewhere in that list, there are five or six that describe exactly what you do, in exactly the structure AI models need.

Go find them.
